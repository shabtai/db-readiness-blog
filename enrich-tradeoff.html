What one question looks like

The benchmark is one 25-table database (a beekeeping operation + a B2B-SaaS audit log + a healthcare-claims system, merged). Questions are asked in plain business language; the agent has to map each onto opaque columns and write the query.

Tableclaims — 10 columns, 13 rows
Columnsclaim_id, patient_id, dos, claim_type, icd10_primary, icd10_secondary, cpt, rendering_npi, paid_amt, service_year
Question“On average, how much are we reimbursed for each billed service event, to two decimals?”
QuerySELECT ROUND(AVG(paid_amt), 2) FROM claims;  →  610.27

Nothing in the question says paid_amt or claims — "reimbursed" → paid_amt, "billed service event" → a claims row. That translation is the work, and it's exactly what low-signal names make expensive.

A harder one, where the decisive fact is in the data, not the question:

Tablehcc_reference — 6 columns, 16 rows (a risk-code crosswalk)
Columnsicd10, description, hcc_code, hcc_description, raf_weight, model
Question“How many distinct risk-adjustment condition categories does our coding crosswalk cover?”
NaïveSELECT COUNT(DISTINCT hcc_code) FROM hcc_reference; — counts all 16 rows, but 6 have a blank hcc_code (they map to nothing).
Correct… WHERE hcc_code <> '';  →  7

The "6 codes map to nothing" rule is written nowhere in the schema — only the enrichment captured it. The brief carries it, so every model answers 7; raw, both frontier models over-count.
